As a software engineer, I deal with information daily. Working across systems of various sizes, I came to understand that absence carries its own signal — a group of servers that stops responding tells you just as much as those that crash loudly. I found this idea — that the absence of information is in itself information — growing on me.

What I was going through at the time, in my personal environment, at my workplace, and in relationships I lost or created, made me realize that this idea is also applicable to interpersonal relationships. The absence of communication with a loved one is information. The lack of interest in an activity is information. The lack of empathy in society is information. Even the lack of trees in the middle of a forest, you guessed it, is information.

This idea, without having read any philosophical book or studied a prominent philosopher, stuck in my mind for a long time. I was trying to express it in a brief but powerful way so that I could relay the message, the understanding of signals as information — or better yet — the lack thereof, but at the same time, leave the interpretation of the information open.

The philosophical implications run deeper - absence as information suggests that reality is defined as much by what isn't there as by what is. It's a reminder that information isn't just about positive signals but about the entire context of expectations, patterns, and deviations from them.

That is a profound truth. In data science, cryptography, psychology, and everyday life, **what *isn't* there is often just as telling as what is.**

We are biologically wired to notice presence—a sound, a flash of light, a new email. But training yourself to notice the *absence* of something is like gaining a superpower.

Here are a few ways "nothing" speaks volumes:

***1. The Classic: Abraham Wald and Survivor Bias***

During World War II, statistician Abraham Wald was asked to determine where to add armor to bomber planes. The military looked at the returning planes, saw bullet holes in the wings and fuselage, and naturally wanted to reinforce those areas.

Wald pointed out they were looking at the wrong data. The armor needed to go where the bullet holes *weren't*—on the engines and cockpit. The planes hit in those areas didn't make it back to be analyzed. **The absence of holes was the real data.**

***2. Sherlock Holmes and the "Dog that Didn't Bark"***

In Arthur Conan Doyle’s story *Silver Blaze*, Sherlock Holmes solves a crime by focusing on what *didn't* happen:

> **Detective:** "Is there any point to which you would wish to draw my attention?"
> **Holmes:** "To the curious incident of the dog in the night-time."
> **Detective:** "The dog did nothing in the night-time."
> **Holmes:** "That was the curious incident."

Because the guard dog didn't bark, Holmes deduced that the intruder was someone the dog knew well. Silence was the clue.

***3. Modern Contexts Where Absence is Data***

* **Communication:** A sudden drop in text frequency from a friend, or a company's total silence on a controversial issue, communicates a massive shift in state.
* **Medicine:** A doctor doesn't just look for what's wrong; the *absence* of certain symptoms is what allows them to rule out deadly misdiagnoses.
* **User Experience (UX):** If users are completely ignoring a bright, shiny new button on a website, that lack of engagement is loud, clear feedback that the design or value proposition is broken.

> **The Takeaway:** When analyzing any situation, always ask yourself: *What am I expecting to see here that is missing?*

“Absence is information” is a concise way of saying that what *doesn't* happen, appear, or get communicated can be meaningful too.

Examples:

* If someone usually replies quickly and suddenly stops responding, that absence may convey information about a change in circumstances, priorities, or feelings.
* In science, failing to detect an expected signal can be evidence against a hypothesis.
* In history, what's missing from records can reveal who had power, whose voices were excluded, or what was intentionally concealed.
* In data analysis, missing values themselves can carry meaning if the pattern of missingness is non-random.

The idea comes up in fields ranging from information theory and statistics to psychology and communication. The key caveat is that absence is often **ambiguous** information: it tells you something has changed or is missing, but not necessarily *why*. Interpreting that absence correctly usually requires context.

So a fuller version might be: **absence is information, but not always explanation.**
